What is phishing?

From Wiktionary:

The act of sending email that falsely claims to be from a legitimate organization. This is usually combined with a threat or request for information: for example, that an account will close, a balance is due, or information is missing from an account. The email will ask the recipient to supply confidential information, such as bank account details, PINs or passwords; these details are then used by the owners of the website to conduct fraud.

How can I tell if I am getting phished?

There are no ways of identifying phishing that work one hundred percent of the time, but there are things you can look out for:

  • A call to immediate action
  • Grammatic or typographic errors
  • Deceptive Hyperlinks

Each of these are explained below.

The Signs of Phishing, Explained

A Call to Immediate Action

Phishing emails will often lure you into acting irrationally by fostering a sense of urgency. They will often do this by threatening the closure of an account, the levy of a hefty fee, legal action or other vexations if you do not click on a link and do something within an short deadline.

This deadline is often far more imminent than most legitimate organizations would impose. For example, most organizations that would charge a fee of some kind would typically give a few days of warning prior to doing so. A phishing email, on the other hand, might say that you have 12 hours to log in to "update your settings," to avoid the charge.

Grammatic or Typographic Errors

Face it: statistically speaking, most people on the planet, and, hence, on the Internet, are not from your home country, and do not speak your native language. Ceteris Paribus, you should expect that emails from foreign countries would have more typographic or grammatic errors.

For that matter, most legitimate organizations that will contact you through email will put their communications through professional scrutiny, which will filter out typographic errors and grammatic errors--the mistakes of a non-native tongue notwithstanding.

Deceptive Hyperlinks

Phishing emails will usually link you to a website via a clickable hyperlink. In most cases, these hyperlinks are blue and underlined to indicate that they are hyperlinks. The text that you see for a hyperlink can differ from the actual thing to which it links.

For instance, a link might read http://good-site.com, but actually direct you to http://bad-site.com. In a typical web browser or email client, when you hover over a hyperlink, you will see the actual thing it links to in a bottom corner of the page. If what you see does not match the domain name of the URL it is linking to, it might be phishing.

Note that it is normal for emails to link you to long URLs that contain a lot of random characters. What really matters is who owns the domain you are being sent to--not the stuff that comes after it.

Below is an example of a deceptive hyperlink. Try hovering over it to see if you can see what it actually links to.

https://www.google.com

Phishing Email Examples

Obvious

            Henlo honey,please to meet you. Your acount will expire in three
            days if you do'nt log in here:
            http://facebook.com.

            Yours In God's Love,
            - facebook company

Passable

            Attention Facebook user! Your account is soon to expire if you do
            not log in and udpate your security settings. Click
            this link to update
            your security settings to avoid the expiration of your account.

            Regards,
            Facebook Cybersecurity department
            Phone: 1-650-308-7300
            1601 Willow Rd. Menlo Park, CA 94025

This email is much less obvious than the first, but it does have one subtle hint: the phisher transposed the letters "d" and "p" in the first occurrence of the word "update." The real Facebook would almost certainly not have such a typo in an email.

Further abnormalities in this email are the fact that this fake Facebook did not call you by name, but rather, just referred to you as "Facebook user." For that matter, the circumstances of the email itself are suspicious: do you really think that Facebook would delete your account just because you did not update some security setting?

Crafty

A truly crafty phishing email will include personal information about you, have no typographic or grammatic errors, and create a believable scenario that requires you to log in. The clever email below actually has two phishing links. 

            Hey Jonathan!

            Your friends are signing up in droves for an event in Tampa
            tomorrow, and you have been invited! Log in to RSVP so you
            do not miss out on this once-in-a-lifetime opportunity!

            - Your Friends at Facebook

            
                Facebook, Inc.
                Phone: +1 (650) 308-7300
                1601 Willow Rd. Menlo Park, CA 94025
                Click here to unsubscribe from these emails.

What do I do if I am getting phished?

At the very least, do not supply your credentials to the website. If you are uncertain if something is phishing, get a second opinion, preferrably from a cybersecurity officer, if your organization has one. If this is not available to you, you can try calling the organization that sent you the email to confirm whether or not it is normal for them to send out such an email. Finally, you should report anything suspicious to your organization's cybersecurity department, if it has one.
